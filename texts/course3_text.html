Course 3

Deriving Requirements using e3value and e3fraud techniques

Lecturer

Prof. Roel Wieringa, Chair of Information Systems at the University of Twente, The Netherlands. Over the past 30 years he has done research in several aspects of formal specification of database constraints, requirements engineering and conceptual modeling for information systems, design of e-business networks, IT security risk assessment, and the design of situation-aware platforms for decentralized coordination. He has written three textbooks, Requirements Engineering, Frameworks for Understanding (Wiley 1996), Design Methods for Reactive Systems (Morgan Kaufmann 2003), and Design Science Methodology for Information Systems and Software Engineering (Springer, 2014). He has been Associate Editor in Chief of IEEE Software for the area of requirements engineering from 2004 to 2007, scientific director of the Dutch national School for Information and Knowledge Systems from 2006 to 2011 and chair of the Computer Science Department of the UT from 2009 to 2012. In 2017 he co-founded TheValueEngineers.nl, which provides software and gives advice about designing sustainable and robust e-business models.

Description

Modern business is done increasingly online in a network of other businesses and consumers. Business processes are intertwined with information technology (IT), and the adoption of new IT such as cloud computing, sensor technology, big data technology and artificial intelligence is often referred to as the digital transformation. In this context, IT requirements are business requirements, and software engineering has become part of business engineering. In this tutorial we present the e3value method to identify IT requirements by creating a business model of online business.

The core discipline is business modeling, which consists of modeling the network of businesses and consumers that exchange objects of value with each other, such as money, goods, services, or experiences (e.g. music). Before IT requirements are identified, this network must be analyzed from an economic perspective on profitability for all business actors, as well as on robustness against online fraud.

Once the business model is shown to be economically robust and sustainable, it must be mapped to requirements on the IT infrastructure that will deliver the required services, and to processes that coordinate services provided by different business actors. This results in three mutually aligned models, namely an e3value business model, an IT architecture model, and a coordination process model. Changes in any one of these models may result in changes in the other two models.

The tutorial presents some illustrative as well as real-world examples of business models, and will demonstrate the free software with which these models can be created and analyzed.
