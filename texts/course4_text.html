Description

The Qualitative Research paradigm focuses on understanding the meaning people attribute to a phenomenon of interest for the researcher. Our goal when performing qualitative study often is to discover, describe, interpret, and understand the phenomenon from the perspective of those being studied. We believe, as researchers engaged in qualitative research in software engineering context, that achieving insight and understanding from the perspective of the practitioners in their working context offers the greatest promises of improving software engineering practice. However, the philosophical foundations and, as a consequence, the methods and tools of qualitative research are very different from those of positivist, the quantitative paradigm that usually underlies controlled experimentation and survey research. The understanding of these epistemological and ontological foundations of qualitative research is important not only for the design and for development of the research, but to interpret and assess the validity of its results.

In this tutorial, I will introduce the basic philosophical foundations that underlie the qualitative research paradigm and then, using practical examples of qualitative studies about the software engineering practice, I intend to describe the most common research methods and tools used to design and conduct qualitative studies.

I will cover the following aspects: the epistemological and ontological foundations of qualitative research; the three main qualitative research methods and their application in software engineering research – basic qualitative research, qualitative case studies, and ethnography; techniques for data collection and analysis; ethical aspects; interpretation of qualitative studies’ results and validity issues.

The presentation will start with a brief discussion on the theoretical foundations. I will then present practical examples of qualitative studies of software practice. At this point, I will encourage the participants to share their research problems, and we will discuss in the group how to apply qualitative research methods to address these problems. Then, I will use a practical example to explain data collection and analysis techniques. Finally, I will stimulate a group discussion about interpretation of research results, application of the findings, and validity issues.

Anyone interested in conducting qualitative research is welcome to this tutorial. Nevertheless, the tutorial will also be of interest for those researchers seeking for a broader understanding of the philosophical foundations of different research paradigms and how these differences influence the design, development, and assessment of empirical studies.
